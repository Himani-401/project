Developmental Milestones

Infants (0-12 months)

  • Grasping objects
  • Babbling and imitating sounds
  • Responding to their name
  • Sitting without support
  • Pulling themselves up to stand
  • Drinking from a cup with assistance
  • Crawling on hands and knees
  • Saying first words (e.g., "mama," "dada")
  • Beginning to feed themselves with fingers
  • Taking first steps (usually around 12 months)

Toddlers (1-3 years)

  • Running and climbing
  • Using utensils to eat
  • Building simple tower structures with blocks
  • Pointing to body parts when asked
  • Following simple instructions
  • Scribbling with crayons
  • Naming familiar objects and pictures
  • Pretend play, such as feeding a doll or talking on a toy phone
  • Engaging in parallel play with other children
  • Beginning to use the toilet independently (potty training)

Preschoolers (3-5 years)

  • Riding a tricycle
  • Dressing and undressing independently (e.g., putting on shoes, zipping up jackets)
  • Speaking clearly and in longer sentences
  • Recognizing and naming colors and shapes
  • Counting and recognizing numbers up to 10
  • Playing cooperatively with other children
  • Cutting with scissors along a straight line
  • Understanding simple concepts of time (e.g., morning, night, yesterday)
  • Showing interest in books and storytelling
  • Following more complex instructions and rules in games

School-Age Children (6-12 years)

  • Riding a bicycle without training wheels
  • Tying shoelaces independently
  • Reading fluently and comprehending texts
  • Writing legibly and forming letters correctly
  • Understanding and using basic math operations (e.g., addition, subtraction)
  • Engaging in organized sports and recreational activities
  • Making and keeping friends
  • Demonstrating empathy and understanding others' perspectives
  • Developing hobbies and interests outside of school
  • Demonstrating independence in completing tasks and responsibilities

Parenting Tips

for infants

is your child crying too much??

  • Comforting Techniques:
    • Try soothing your baby by rocking them gently.
    • Sing or play calming music to your baby.
    • Offer a pacifier to help soothe them.
  • Swaddling: Wrap your baby snugly in a blanket to help them feel secure and calm.
  • Check for Hunger or Discomfort:
    • Ensure your baby isn't hungry.
    • Check for a wet diaper or discomfort.
    • Address any pain from gas or colic.
  • Skin-to-Skin Contact: Hold your baby close to your chest for warmth and reassurance.

Is your chikd not eating??

  • Establish a Routine:
    • Stick to regular feeding times.
    • Offer breast or bottle when your baby shows hunger cues.
  • Try Different Positions: Experiment with breastfeeding positions or bottle-feeding techniques.
  • Burping: Burp your baby frequently during and after feedings to prevent gas.
  • Consult a Pediatrician: Seek advice if your baby refuses feedings or has difficulty latching.

is your child not sleeping??

  • Establish a Bedtime Routine:
    • Develop a calming routine signaling bedtime.
    • Include activities like bathing, reading, and gentle rocking.
  • Create a Comfortable Environment:
    • Ensure a comfortable crib with dim lighting.
    • Maintain a consistent temperature for sleep.
  • Safe Sleep Practices:
    • Always place your baby on their back to sleep.
    • Avoid loose bedding or soft toys in the crib.

for toddlers

Dealing with Tantrums:

  • Stay Calm: Keep your composure and avoid reacting impulsively to tantrums.
  • Offer Choices: Provide your toddler with limited choices to help them feel a sense of control and autonomy.
  • Use Distraction: Redirect your toddler's attention to something else to help diffuse tantrums.
  • Set Clear Limits: Establish clear and consistent boundaries to help your toddler understand acceptable behavior.

Encouraging Healthy Eating Habits:

  • Provide Nutritious Options: Offer a variety of healthy foods, including fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.
  • Model Healthy Eating: Be a role model by eating nutritious foods yourself and involving your toddler in meal preparation.
  • Offer Small, Frequent Meals: Toddlers may prefer smaller, more frequent meals and snacks rather than large meals.
  • Avoid Power Struggles: Offer healthy choices and let your toddler decide how much to eat. Avoid forcing or bribing them to eat.

Promoting Positive Sleep Habits:

  • Establish a Bedtime Routine: Create a consistent bedtime routine to signal to your toddler that it's time for sleep.
  • Create a Calming Environment: Ensure your toddler's bedroom is quiet, dark, and comfortable for sleep.
  • Limit Screen Time: Avoid screens (TV, tablets, phones) before bedtime as they can interfere with sleep.
  • Address Nighttime Fears: Offer reassurance and comfort if your toddler wakes up frightened during the night.

for teenagers

Dealing with Stress:

  • Practice Stress-Relief Techniques: Encourage your teenager to try relaxation techniques such as deep breathing, meditation, or yoga.
  • Promote Healthy Habits: Advocate for regular exercise, proper nutrition, and adequate sleep to help manage stress levels.
  • Open Communication: Keep communication lines open and encourage your teenager to express their feelings and concerns.
  • Encourage Time Management: Help your teenager develop effective time management skills to balance academics, extracurricular activities, and personal time.

Building Resilience:

  • Encourage Problem-Solving: Teach your teenager problem-solving skills to navigate challenges and setbacks effectively.
  • Promote Positive Self-Talk: Help your teenager develop a positive mindset and practice self-compassion in the face of adversity.
  • Set Realistic Goals: Encourage your teenager to set realistic and achievable goals, celebrating their progress along the way.
  • Seek Support: Encourage your teenager to seek support from trusted friends, family members, or counselors during difficult times.

Managing Peer Pressure:

  • Discuss Peer Pressure: Have open and honest conversations about peer pressure and its potential impact on decision-making.
  • Role-Playing Scenarios: Practice assertiveness skills and refusal strategies through role-playing scenarios with your teenager.
  • Encourage Individuality: Foster a sense of individuality and self-confidence in your teenager to resist negative peer influences.
  • Set Boundaries: Establish clear boundaries and expectations regarding behavior and decision-making with your teenager.
